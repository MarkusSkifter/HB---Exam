\section*{Question 5}

\paragraph{(a)} We modify equation (3) only. The budget constraint becomes
\[
  c_t = \bigl(1 - \tau - \tau_1 \cdot \mathbf{1}(y_t \geq 8)\bigr)\, w_t\, l_t + \pi_t \cdot \mathbf{1}(l_t = 0), \tag{3$'$}
\]
where $\tau_1 \geq 0$ is the additional tax rate applied from year 8 onward ($y_t \geq 8$ in 1-indexed PDF notation, implemented as \texttt{(year+1) >= 8} in 0-indexed code). All other equations are unchanged. The government budget term becomes $G_{i,t} = (\tau + \tau_1 \cdot \mathbf{1}(y_{i,t} \geq 8))\,w_{i,t}\,l_{i,t} - \pi_t\cdot\mathbf{1}(l_{i,t}=0)$.

\paragraph{(b)}

\begin{figure}[H]

  \caption{Government budget balance $G(\theta)$ as a function of $\tau_1 \in [0.015,\, 0.025]$ with $\pi_1 = 0.6$ fixed. The dashed line marks $G_3 = 811{,}084$.}
  \label{fig:q5_search}
\end{figure}

$G(\theta)$ reaches its maximum within the search interval at $\tau_1^* = 0.0235$, giving $G(\theta_5^*) = 742{,}744$ (a remaining gap of $68{,}340$ relative to $G_3$). The curve is non-monotone: a rising direct-revenue effect is interrupted by discrete threshold crossings where bundles of marginal workers exit employment, sharply reducing the tax base. Even at $\tau_1^* = 0.0235$, the reform cannot close the budget gap because the retirement subsidy $\pi_1 = 0.6$ induces large-scale labor-force exit that no moderate additional tax can fully offset.

\paragraph{(c)} We measure individual welfare as realized discounted lifetime utility:
\[
  W_i = \sum_{t=0}^{T-1} \beta^t\, U(c_{i,t},\, l_{i,t}),
\]
where $U$ is as in equation (9). This is computed directly from the simulated consumption and labor-supply paths and is expressed in the same units as the model's value function.

\paragraph{(d)}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../figures/q5_welfare_by_cohort.pdf}
  \caption{Welfare by birth cohort under $\theta_5^*$ relative to the baseline. Panel (a): mean welfare gain. Panel (b): share of workers better off.}
  \label{fig:q5_welfare}

The reform is not Pareto-improving for any cohort. Only 23\% of the lowest-wage cohort ($b=0$) gains, as they switch to non-employment and collect $\pi_1$. The remaining 77\% in $b=0$ and essentially all workers in $b=1$--$4$ lose: they remain employed, pay $\tau_1$ from year 8, but receive no retirement subsidy. Higher-wage cohorts ($b=2,3,4$) are almost uniformly hurt since their $\alpha_b$ values make continued employment optimal at any plausible $\tau_1$. The mean welfare losses range from roughly $-0.26$ (cohorts $b=2,3$) to $-0.61$ (cohort $b=0$, averaging over beneficiaries and losers).
