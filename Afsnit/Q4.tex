\section*{Question 4}

\paragraph{(a)} We modify equation (7) only. The non-employment income becomes
\[
  \pi_t = \pi_0 + \pi_1 \cdot \mathbf{1}(t \geq 10) \cdot \mathbf{1}(y_t \geq 8), \tag{7$'$}
\]
where $\pi_1 = 0.6$ and $y_t = t + b$. All other equations (1)--(6), (8)--(11) are unchanged. Because workers have perfect foresight, equation (7$'$) enters the Bellman equation from period 1 onward---workers in years before 8 already account for the future subsidy when optimising.

\textit{Implementation note:} the PDF uses 1-indexed age ($t \in \{1,\ldots,T\}$) and year, while the code uses 0-indexed variables. To keep the threshold numbers readable and matching the PDF, the conditions are written as \texttt{(t+1) >= 10} and \texttt{(year+1) >= 8} in the code.

\paragraph{(b)}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../figures/q4_age_profiles.pdf}
  \caption{Age profiles for the baseline ($\pi_1 = 0$) and the reform ($\pi_1 = 0.6$).}
  \label{fig:q4_age}

Average labor supply is nearly identical for ages 1--10, with only a small anticipation effect visible under the reform. From age 11 onward, labor supply falls substantially as the additional subsidy makes non-employment attractive. The employment rate stays at 100\% in the baseline throughout; under the reform it begins to drop at age 11 and reaches roughly 89\% by age 20---an 11 percentage-point gap driven by workers who prefer the higher $\pi_1$ subsidy over their diminishing after-tax wage late in the life cycle.

The two models track each other closely before year 8. From year 8 onward the reform diverges downward: the additional subsidy $\pi_1$ raises expenditures for older non-employed workers while the reduction in employment shrinks the tax base, so net government revenue falls relative to the baseline throughout the remainder of the simulation.
