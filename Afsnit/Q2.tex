\section*{Question 2}

We solve and simulate two model variants: the baseline with $\tau = 0.3$ and a counterfactual with no labor income tax ($\tau = 0$). Note that the cohort wage constants are corrected to $(\alpha_0,\alpha_1,\alpha_2,\alpha_3,\alpha_4) = (0.7, 0.8, 0.9, 1.1, 1.2)$ as specified, replacing the linear interpolation in the starter code.

With no labor income tax, workers supply more hours throughout the life cycle since the net return to work is higher. The employment rate mirrors this pattern: positive $\tau$ pushes marginal workers into non-employment to collect $\pi_0$. Consumption is higher under $\tau = 0$ both because workers retain their full earnings and because greater labor supply builds more human capital, compounding the wage advantage over the life cycle.
