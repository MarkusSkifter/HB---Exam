\section*{Question 3}

The government budget balance is $G(\theta) = \sum_i \sum_t G_{i,t}$, where $G_{i,t} = \tau w_{i,t} l_{i,t} - \pi_0 \cdot \mathbf{1}(l_{i,t} = 0)$ is tax revenue net of non-employment subsidies for individual $i$ at age $t$.

\paragraph{(a)} For the baseline policy parameters $\theta = (\pi_0 = 0.5,\, \tau = 0.3)$:
\[
  G_3 = G(\theta) = 811{,}084
\]

\paragraph{(b)} Figure~\ref{fig:q3} plots $G(\theta)$ across the full tax range. Revenue rises until approximately $\tau = 0.7$, then collapses as mass exit into non-employment flips the government from tax collector to net payer of subsidies.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.7\textwidth]{../figures/q3_laffer.pdf}
  \caption{Government budget balance $G(\theta)$ as a function of the tax rate $\tau$. The dashed line marks $G_3$.}
  \label{fig:q3}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{(c)} The budget balance rises monotonically until $\tau = 0.7$, where it peaks at roughly $1{,}993{,}383$, then collapses. At $\tau = 0$, $G = 0$ exactly: everyone works, so there is no tax revenue and no subsidy expenditure. As $\tau$ rises, revenue grows because the tax rate effect on each hour worked outweighs the modest behavioral response. The model features a sharp tipping point around $\tau = 0.8$: once the net wage falls enough that non-employment income ($\pi_0 = 0.5$) becomes competitive, large numbers of workers exit employment simultaneously. At $\tau = 0.9$, $G = -500{,}000 = -\pi_0 \times N \times T$, meaning essentially everyone is non-employed across all periods and the government pays the full subsidy bill with zero tax receipts. The late peak (at $\tau = 0.7$ rather than, say, 0.3) reflects the high productivity gains from human capital accumulation: wages are large enough that working remains worthwhile even under heavy taxation, until the tax rate crosses a threshold where it no longer pays.
